Recap and the Wave Equation

Where we left off

Week 1 closed with the run-time recursion:

\[ \begin{aligned} \mathbf h_{n+1} &= \mathbf A\, \mathbf h_n + \mathbf b\, f_n \\ \mathbf y_n &= \mathbf C\, \mathbf h_n \end{aligned} \]

But we never actually built \(\mathbf A\).

This week: two recipes to construct \(\mathbf A\) from the PDE. They differ in the basis we choose to represent the spatial field.

The 1D Wave Equation

The ideal vibrating string (week 1):

\[\tvar{\partial^2_t}\, w = \hat T^2\, \op{\partial^2_x}\, w, \quad x \in [0, 1]\]

State is the field \(w(x,t)\) – a function of both space and time. Time evolution and spatial shape are tangled together in this PDE.

Plan: separate space from time, expand each in a basis of simple functions, then re-assemble.

Separation of Variables

Basis Expansion

The superposition is already a basis expansion in space. Rename for the rest of the lecture:

\[F_\mu(x) \;\longrightarrow\; \phi_\mu(x), \qquad G_\mu(t) \;\longrightarrow\; q_\mu(t)\]

\[\boxed{\;w(x,t) = \sum_\mu \phi_\mu(x)\, q_\mu(t)\;}\]

Claim: functions in the relevant function space can be represented exactly with an infinite complete basis.

Here: finite-energy string shapes on \([0,L]\) that satisfy the boundary conditions. Smooth functions can be approximated with a finite set. The smoother the function, the fewer terms you need.

The choice of spatial representation is what distinguishes modal synthesis (global eigenfunctions) from FDTD (local grid samples, or local interpolation functions if we use basis language). The amplitudes \(q_\mu(t)\) obey ODEs we derive from the PDE – time discretisation comes later, when we do FDTD time-stepping.

Separation + Basis Expansion

Local vs Global Basis Functions

A Teaser: Learn the Basis

Modal and FDTD both pick a fixed spatial basis. A shallow fully-connected network with one hidden layer is itself a basis expansion:

\[\phi_n(x) = \sum_{j=1}^{m} A_{nj}\, \sigma(w_j x + b_j)\]

Activation \(\sigma\) Basis shape Character
ReLU piecewise-linear segments (3+ neurons build a hat) local-capable
sin sinusoid at learned frequency \(w_j\) global-like
tanh, GELU, … smooth localised bumps mixed

With \(\sigma = \sin\) this is a SIREN trunk: a finite sinusoidal dictionary with frequencies \(w_j\) and phases \(b_j\) as learnable parameters. The analytical sine basis \(\sin(\mu\pi x/L)\) is a special case with fixed integer frequencies and zero phase. Here the optimiser is free to pick non-integer \(w_j\) and arbitrary \(b_j\).

A Teaser: From Network to Modal ODEs

week02/notebooks/modal_identification.py fits the modal expansion directly to grid data \(U \in \mathbb R^{N_x \times T}\):

\[u(x, t) = \sum_{n=1}^{N} \phi_n(x;\theta)\, \bigl[\, e^{-\gamma_n t}\!\left(a_n\cos\omega_n t + b_n\sin\omega_n t\right)\bigr]\]

Jointly identified:

  • spatial modes \(\phi_n(x;\theta)\): shallow sin-network multiplied by \(x(L-x)/L^2\) so Dirichlet ends are exact
  • modal frequencies \(\omega_n\), damping \(\gamma_n = \mathrm{softplus}(\tilde\gamma_n) \ge 0\) (poles \(s_n = -\gamma_n \pm i\omega_n\) stay in the left half \(s\)-plane)
  • amplitudes \(a_n, b_n\) encoding the initial condition

The analytical sine basis is a hard-coded special case with fixed \(w_j\) and \(b_j\). The network lets the data choose those numbers.

Two Ways to Discretise

Two Strategies for \(\mathcal L\)

The spatial operator \(\mathcal L = \hat T^2\, \op{\partial^2_x}\) (later: \(\hat T^2 \op{\partial^2_x} - \hat D^2 \op{\partial^4_x}\), \(\Delta\), \(\Delta^2\), …) is what we need to represent on a computer.

Project the PDE (Modal)

Expand \(w(x,t) = \sum_\mu \phi_\mu(x)\, q_\mu(t)\) and project the PDE onto \(\{\phi_\mu\}\).

Pick \(\phi_\mu\) = eigenfunctions of the spatial derivative operator \(\Rightarrow\) operator becomes scalar multiplication by \(-\eig{\lambda_\mu^2}\).

State: mode amplitudes \(q_\mu(t)\).

Discretise the operator (FDTD)

Sample the field at grid points \(x_i\) and replace \(\partial^2_x\) with a finite-difference stencil.

\(\mathcal L\) becomes a sparse banded matrix acting on the vector \(\mathbf w(t) = [w(x_i, t)]\).

State: grid values \(w(x_i, t)\).

Today: modal first, FDTD second.

And Time? Finite Differences Either Way

Both routes leave us with second-order ODEs in time:

  • Modal: \(\ddot q_\mu + 2\gamma_\mu \dot q_\mu + \omega_\mu^2 q_\mu = b_\mu f(t)\) (one scalar ODE per mode)
  • FDTD: \(\ddot{\mathbf w}(t) = \hat T^2\, \mathbf D_{xx}\, \mathbf w(t) + \mathbf b\, f(t)\) (coupled grid ODE)

We discretise time the same way we discretised space (in the FDTD case): replace \(\partial^2_t\) with a centred second difference.

Störmer-Verlet / leapfrog for \(\ddot q = F(q)\):

\[\frac{q^{n+1} - 2 q^n + q^{n-1}}{\Delta t^2} = F(q^n) \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad q^{n+1} = 2 q^n - q^{n-1} + \Delta t^2\, F(q^n)\]

Two-step, explicit, symplectic for conservative systems. Stack \((q^n, q^{n-1})\) and we are back at \(\mathbf h_{n+1} = \mathbf A\, \mathbf h_n + \mathbf b\, f_n\) from week 1.

The Spatial Operator: Modal View

Eigenvalue Problem

We want a basis that makes the spatial derivative operator \(\op{\partial^2_x}\) act diagonally. Pick \(\xvar{\phi_\mu}\) to satisfy

\[\op{\partial^2_x}\, \xvar{\phi_\mu(x)} \;=\; -\,\eig{\lambda_\mu^2}\, \xvar{\phi_\mu(x)}\]

operator acting on an eigenfunction returns the same eigenfunction scaled by an eigenvalue (squared, so that \(\eig{\lambda_\mu}\) carries units of a wave number).

Boundary conditions are part of the operator – they pick which \(\xvar{\phi_\mu}\) are admissible. Dirichlet, Neumann, mixed all give different spectra.

Ideal Dirichlet string (\(\xvar{\phi_\mu(0) = \phi_\mu(L) = 0}\)):

\[\xvar{\phi_\mu(x) = \sin\!\left(\frac{\mu\pi x}{L}\right)}, \qquad \eig{\lambda_\mu = \frac{\mu\pi}{L}}, \qquad \mu = 1, 2, 3, \dots\]

The natural frequencies are \(\tvar{\omega_\mu} = \hat T\, \eig{\lambda_\mu} = \hat T\, \mu\pi/L\) – a harmonic series.

Mode Shapes

Note

Animation placeholder #3. First five sine modes \(\phi_\mu(x)\) drawn one at a time with labels \(\mu\) and \(\omega_\mu\), then plotted together. File: images/mode_shapes_buildup.mp4.

The \(\phi_\mu\) are orthogonal under the standard inner product on \([0, L]\):

\[\langle \phi_\mu, \phi_\nu \rangle = \int_0^L \phi_\mu(x)\, \phi_\nu(x)\, dx = \tfrac{L}{2}\, \delta_{\mu\nu}\]

This is how we extract coefficients \(a_\mu\) from a given shape: project onto the basis.

Generalise the Operator: Add Stiffness

The stiff string from week 1:

\[\tvar{\partial^2_t}\, w \;=\; \hat T^2\, \op{\partial^2_x}\, w \;-\; \hat D^2\, \op{\partial^4_x}\, w\]

The spatial operator gains a fourth derivative.

Same recipe, same boundary conditions. Sines still diagonalise both pieces:

\[\op{\partial^2_x}\, \xvar{\phi_\mu} = -\,\eig{\lambda_\mu^2}\, \xvar{\phi_\mu}, \qquad \op{\partial^4_x}\, \xvar{\phi_\mu} = +\,\eig{\lambda_\mu^4}\, \xvar{\phi_\mu}\]

For simply-supported ends the eigenfunctions are still sines, but the spectrum shifts:

\[\tvar{\omega_\mu^2} \;=\; \hat T^2\, \eig{\lambda_\mu^2} \;+\; \hat D^2\, \eig{\lambda_\mu^4}, \qquad \eig{\lambda_\mu} = \frac{\mu\pi}{L}\]

The high modes are pushed up disproportionately \(\to\) inharmonicity.

Stiffness \(\to\) Inharmonicity

Modal ODEs and State-space Form

Projection onto the Modal Basis

Inverse transform – sum over the discrete mode index \(\mu\):

\[w(x,t) = \sum_\mu \xvar{\phi_\mu(x)}\, \tvar{q_\mu(t)}\]

Forward transform – integrate the field against each mode:

\[\begin{aligned} \tvar{q_\mu(t)} &= \frac{1}{N_\mu} \int_\Omega w(x,t)\, \xvar{\phi_\mu(x)}\, dx \\[4pt] &= \frac{1}{N_\mu}\, \bigl\langle w(\cdot, t),\, \xvar{\phi_\mu} \bigr\rangle \end{aligned}\]

Two readings of the same operation:

  • Projection of \(w(\cdot, t)\) onto \(\xvar{\phi_\mu}\) – orthogonality of \(\{\xvar{\phi_\mu}\}\) makes the picture exact.
  • Integral transform with kernel \(\xvar{\phi_\mu(x)}\). Here \(\xvar{\phi_\mu(x) = \sin(\mu\pi x/L)}\), so this is (a discrete-mode) sine transform.

Projection onto the Modal Basis

Insert \(w(x,t) = \sum_\mu \phi_\mu(x)\, q_\mu(t)\) into the forced damped wave equation:

\[\sum_\mu \phi_\mu(x)\, \ddot q_\mu(t) = \hat T^2 \sum_\mu \partial^2_x\phi_\mu(x)\, q_\mu(t) + \dots\]

Use \(\op{\partial^2_x}\xvar{\phi_\mu} = -\eig{\lambda_\mu^2}\xvar{\phi_\mu}\), take the inner product with \(\phi_\nu\), and orthogonality kills the cross-terms:

\[\boxed{\;\ddot q_\mu + 2\gamma_\mu\, \dot q_\mu + \omega_\mu^2\, q_\mu = b_\mu\, f(t)\;}\]

Quantity Role
\(\tvar{\omega_\mu^2} = \hat T^2 \eig{\lambda_\mu^2} \,(+ \hat D^2 \eig{\lambda_\mu^4})\) Modal frequency squared
\(2\gamma_\mu\) Modal damping coefficient (next slide)
\(b_\mu = \phi_\mu(x_\text{in}) / \lVert \phi_\mu \rVert^2\) Input projection
\(q_\mu(t)\) Modal amplitude (scalar ODE per mode)

Damping per Mode

Each loss term from week 1 projects cleanly onto the modal basis.

PDE term Modal contribution to \(2\gamma_\mu\)
\(-\hat d_1 \, \partial_t w\) \(2\gamma_\mu \mathrel{+}= \hat d_1\)
\(+\hat d_3 \, \partial_t \op{\partial^2_x} w\) \(2\gamma_\mu \mathrel{+}= \hat d_3 \eig{\lambda_\mu^2}\)

So \(2\gamma_\mu = \hat d_1 + \hat d_3\, \eig{\lambda_\mu^2}\).

Frequency-dependent damping (\(\hat d_3\)) becomes a per-mode damping coefficient – for free, just from the projection. High modes decay faster, exactly what real strings do.

Output Map and State-space Form

The output at readout position \(x_\text{out}\) is the field sampled at that point:

\[y(t) = w(x_\text{out}, t) = \sum_\mu \phi_\mu(x_\text{out})\, q_\mu(t)\]

Stack the modal amplitudes and their derivatives into a state vector:

\[\mathbf h = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf q \\ \dot{\mathbf q} \end{bmatrix} \in \mathbb R^{2N}, \qquad \dot{\mathbf h} = \mathbf A\, \mathbf h + \mathbf b\, f(t), \qquad y = \mathbf C\, \mathbf h\]

\[\mathbf A = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf 0 & \mathbf I \\ -\boldsymbol\Omega^2 & -2\boldsymbol\Gamma \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \boldsymbol\Omega = \mathrm{diag}(\omega_\mu), \quad \boldsymbol\Gamma = \mathrm{diag}(\gamma_\mu)\]

Here \(\boldsymbol\Omega\) and \(\boldsymbol\Gamma\) are real diagonal matrices; complex modal poles appear as eigenvalues of \(\mathbf A\), not as entries of \(\boldsymbol\Omega\).

For a point force at \(x_\text{in}\) and one readout at \(x_\text{out}\):

\[\mathbf b = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf 0 \\ \mathbf b_q \end{bmatrix}, \qquad (\mathbf b_q)_\mu = \frac{\phi_\mu(x_\text{in})}{\lVert \phi_\mu\rVert^2}, \qquad \mathbf C = \begin{bmatrix} \phi_1(x_\text{out}) & \cdots & \phi_N(x_\text{out}) & 0 & \cdots & 0 \end{bmatrix}\]

\(\mathbf A\) is block-diagonal in mode pairs \(\to\) the \(N\) modal ODEs decouple completely. Embarrassingly parallel.

2D Preview: Membranes and Plates

Same Recipe, New Domain

The recipe is identical: solve the eigenvalue problem for the spatial operator on the new domain.

Square Dirichlet membrane (\(\partial^2_t w = \hat T^2 \Delta w\) on \([0, L_x]\times[0, L_y]\)):

\[\phi_{m,n}(x,y) = \sin\!\left(\frac{m\pi x}{L_x}\right)\sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi y}{L_y}\right), \quad \lambda_{m,n}^2 = \pi^2\!\left(\frac{m^2}{L_x^2} + \frac{n^2}{L_y^2}\right)\]

Separable in 2D \(\to\) eigenfunctions are products of 1D eigenfunctions.

The frequencies are

\[\omega_{m,n} = \hat T\,\pi\sqrt{\frac{m^2}{L_x^2} + \frac{n^2}{L_y^2}}\]

so they are not integer-spaced in general: they depend on two integer indices through a square root, rather than one integer harmonic number.

Membrane Modes

Circular membranes – not separable in \((x, y)\), but separable in \((r, \theta)\). Eigenfunctions are Bessel functions \(J_n(\lambda_{n,k} r)\cos(n\theta)\), with sine/cosine angular pairs for \(n > 0\). Familiar territory: timpani.

Plates

A plate is to a membrane what a stiff string is to an ideal string: bending stiffness dominates.

The spatial operator becomes biharmonic:

\[\mathcal L = -D\, \Delta^2 = -D \left(\partial^2_x + \partial^2_y\right)^2\]

For a simply-supported rectangular plate:

\[\omega_{m,n}^2 = \frac{D}{\rho h} \left[\pi^2\!\left(\frac{m^2}{L_x^2} + \frac{n^2}{L_y^2}\right)\right]^2\]

The squared bracket gives the cymbal-like inharmonicity: very dense, irregular spectra. Real-world: gongs, cymbals, drums with a stiff head.

Same recipe – different operator, different spectrum, same code structure.

FDTD: The Grid View

Back to the Spatial Operator

We picked the modal basis to make \(\mathcal L\) diagonal.

What if we pick a local grid basis instead?

Sample the field at \(N\) equispaced points \(x_i = (i-1)\, \Delta x\), \(i = 1, \dots, N\), with \(\Delta x = L/(N-1)\):

\[\mathbf w(t) = \begin{bmatrix} w(x_1, t) \\ w(x_2, t) \\ \vdots \\ w(x_N, t) \end{bmatrix} \in \mathbb R^N\]

The degrees of freedom are no longer modal amplitudes \(q_\mu(t)\). They are nodal values \(w_i(t) = w(x_i,t)\).

If we want a basis-language analogy, this corresponds to local interpolation functions \(\Lambda_i(x)\), each centred at grid point \(x_i\).

Discrete Spatial Operator

Approximate \(\partial^2_x w\) by the centred second difference:

\[\partial^2_x w(x_i, t) \approx \frac{w_{i-1} - 2 w_i + w_{i+1}}{\Delta x^2}\]

In matrix form, \(\partial^2_x \mapsto \mathbf D_{xx}\) – stencil \(\tfrac{1}{\Delta x^2}[1, -2, 1]\) on the interior rows:

\[\mathbf D_{xx} = \frac{1}{\Delta x^2}\, \begin{bmatrix} \star & \star & \star & & \\ 1 & -2 & 1 & & \\ & \ddots & \ddots & \ddots & \\ & & 1 & -2 & 1 \\ & & \star & \star & \star \end{bmatrix}\]

The spatial operator is now \(\mathbf D_{xx}\), a sparse banded matrix, not diagonal.

Discrete Spatial Operator

The interior stencil is fixed:

\[\left(\mathbf D_{xx}\mathbf w\right)_i = \frac{w_{i-1} - 2w_i + w_{i+1}}{\Delta x^2}, \qquad i = 2,\dots,N-1\]

The boundary rows of \(\mathbf D_{xx}\) depend on the boundary condition:

Boundary condition Boundary closure
Dirichlet First and last rows are zero; enforce \(w_1^n = w_N^n = 0\) for all \(n\).
Neumann Mirror ghost points: \(w_0 = w_2\), \(w_{N+1} = w_{N-1}\).
Periodic Wrap the stencil so \(w_0 = w_N\) and \(w_{N+1} = w_1\).

So \(\mathbf D_{xx}\) is the interior stencil plus a boundary closure, not just the three numbers \([1,-2,1]\).

Discrete Time: Centred Differences

Approximate \(\partial^2_t w\) the same way – the \(\delta_{tt}\) operator from week 1’s homework:

\[\partial^2_t w(x_i, t_n) \approx \frac{w_i^{n-1} - 2 w_i^n + w_i^{n+1}}{\Delta t^2}\]

Substitute into the wave equation and solve for \(\mathbf w^{n+1}\):

\[\boxed{\;\mathbf w^{n+1} = 2\mathbf w^n - \mathbf w^{n-1} + \left(\hat T\, \frac{\Delta t}{\Delta x}\right)^2 \Delta x^2\, \mathbf D_{xx}\, \mathbf w^n\;}\]

A two-step recursion. The Courant number \(\nu := \hat T\, \Delta t / \Delta x\) controls everything about the scheme.

Output Map and State-space Form

Stack \(\mathbf w^n\) and \(\mathbf w^{n-1}\) into a state vector:

\[\mathbf h_n = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf w^n \\ \mathbf w^{n-1} \end{bmatrix} \in \mathbb R^{2N}, \qquad \mathbf h_{n+1} = \mathbf A\, \mathbf h_n + \mathbf b\, f_n\]

\[\mathbf A = \begin{bmatrix} 2\mathbf I + \nu^2\, \Delta x^2\, \mathbf D_{xx} & -\mathbf I \\ \mathbf I & \mathbf 0 \end{bmatrix}\]

For a point force injected at \(x_\text{in}\), let \(\mathbf g_\text{in}\) be the vector of local interpolation weights, \((\mathbf g_\text{in})_i = \Lambda_i(x_\text{in})\):

\[\mathbf b = \begin{bmatrix} \Delta t^2\,\mathbf g_\text{in} \\ \mathbf 0 \end{bmatrix}\]

For one readout at \(x_\text{out}\), use the same interpolation idea: \((\mathbf g_\text{out})_i = \Lambda_i(x_\text{out})\).

\[\mathbf C = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf g_\text{out}^\top & \mathbf 0^\top \end{bmatrix}\]

Same shape as the modal recursion. \(\mathbf A\) is sparse-banded instead of block-diagonal. The state-space machinery from week 1 carries over unchanged.

Stability: the CFL Condition

The eigenvalues of \(\mathbf D_{xx}\) determine when the explicit scheme blows up.

Plugging an eigenvector \(\mathbf v_k\) into the recursion and demanding \(|z_k| \le 1\) for the per-mode amplification factor yields the Courant–Friedrichs–Lewy condition:

\[\boxed{\;\nu = \hat T\, \frac{\Delta t}{\Delta x} \;\le\; 1\;}\]

  • \(\nu < 1\): stable, but extra numerical dispersion.
  • \(\nu = 1\): the “magic” choice – the discrete scheme is exact for the ideal wave equation.
  • \(\nu > 1\): catastrophic blow-up within milliseconds.

CFL Blow-up

Dispersion in the High Modes

Let \(\lambda\) be the continuous wavenumber, so \(\lambda=\lambda_\mu\) for modal mode \(\mu\).

The modal stiff-string dispersion relation is the physical one:

\[\omega_\text{modal}^2(\lambda) = \hat T^2 \lambda^2 + \hat D^2 \lambda^4\]

For the ideal-string FDTD scheme (\(\hat D=0\)), centred differences give the numerical relation

\[\omega_\text{discrete}(\lambda) = \frac{2}{\Delta t}\arcsin\!\left(\nu\, \sin\!\frac{\lambda\Delta x}{2}\right), \qquad \nu = \hat T\,\frac{\Delta t}{\Delta x}\]

Low modes (\(\lambda\Delta x \ll 1\)): \(\sin(\lambda\Delta x/2) \approx \lambda\Delta x/2\), so \(\omega_\text{discrete} \approx \hat T\,\lambda\), matching the ideal modal relation.

High modes: modal stiffness adds the physical upward bend \(+\hat D^2 \lambda^4\), while FDTD adds numerical dispersion because the grid replaces \(\lambda^2\) by

\[\tilde\lambda^2 = \left(\frac{2}{\Delta x}\sin\!\frac{\lambda\Delta x}{2}\right)^2\]

This is exactly the disagreement the stretch goal exposed: the high eigenvalues of \(\mathbf D_{xx}\) differ from \(-\lambda_\mu^2 = -(\mu\pi/L)^2\).

Modal vs FDTD

State-space Side by Side

Modal: continuous time

\[\mathbf h = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf q \\ \dot{\mathbf q} \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \dot{\mathbf h} = \mathbf A\,\mathbf h + \mathbf b\,f(t), \qquad y = \mathbf C\,\mathbf h\]

\(\mathbf A\) matrix

\[\mathbf A = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf 0 & \mathbf I \\ -\boldsymbol\Omega^2 & -2\boldsymbol\Gamma \end{bmatrix}\]

Diagonal mode pairs: modes are decoupled.

Input map

\[\mathbf b = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf 0 \\ \mathbf b_q \end{bmatrix}, \qquad (\mathbf b_q)_\mu = \frac{\phi_\mu(x_\text{in})}{\lVert\phi_\mu\rVert^2}\]

Input is projected onto eigenfunctions.

Output map

\[\mathbf C = \begin{bmatrix} \phi_1(x_\text{out}) & \cdots & \phi_N(x_\text{out}) & 0 & \cdots & 0 \end{bmatrix}\]

Output samples the modal expansion.

FDTD: discrete time

\[\mathbf h_n = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf w^n \\ \mathbf w^{n-1} \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf h_{n+1} = \mathbf A\,\mathbf h_n + \mathbf b\,f_n, \qquad y_n = \mathbf C\,\mathbf h_n\]

\(\mathbf A\) matrix

\[\mathbf A = \begin{bmatrix} 2\mathbf I + \nu^2\Delta x^2\mathbf D_{xx} & -\mathbf I \\ \mathbf I & \mathbf 0 \end{bmatrix}\]

Sparse grid coupling through \(\mathbf D_{xx}\).

Input map

\[\mathbf b = \begin{bmatrix} \Delta t^2\,\mathbf g_\text{in} \\ \mathbf 0 \end{bmatrix}\]

Input is distributed to nearby grid points.

Output map

\[\mathbf C = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf g_\text{out}^\top & \mathbf 0^\top \end{bmatrix}\]

Output interpolates from local grid values.

Same state-space ingredients, but not the same time domain yet: modal is still an ODE, FDTD is already a recurrence.

Time-discrete modal

Modal: discrete time

\[\mathbf h_n = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf q^n \\ \mathbf q^{n-1} \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf h_{n+1} = \mathbf A\,\mathbf h_n + \mathbf b\,f_n, \qquad y_n = \mathbf C\,\mathbf h_n\]

\(\mathbf A\) matrix

Using centred differences for both \(\ddot{\mathbf q}\) and \(\dot{\mathbf q}\), let \(\mathbf R = (\mathbf I+\Delta t\,\boldsymbol\Gamma)^{-1}\):

\[\mathbf A = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf R(2\mathbf I-\Delta t^2\boldsymbol\Omega^2) & -\mathbf R(\mathbf I-\Delta t\,\boldsymbol\Gamma) \\ \mathbf I & \mathbf 0 \end{bmatrix}\]

Still diagonal mode pairs: the inverse is elementwise, and modes remain decoupled.

\[\mathbf b = \begin{bmatrix} \Delta t^2\mathbf R\mathbf b_q \\ \mathbf 0 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad (\mathbf b_q)_\mu = \frac{\phi_\mu(x_\text{in})}{\lVert\phi_\mu\rVert^2}\]

Input is projected onto eigenfunctions.

Output map

\[\mathbf C = \begin{bmatrix} \phi_1(x_\text{out}) & \cdots & \phi_N(x_\text{out}) & 0 & \cdots & 0 \end{bmatrix}\]

Output samples the modal expansion.

\[\mathbf C = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf g_\text{out}^\top & \mathbf 0^\top \end{bmatrix}\]

Output interpolates from local grid values.

Same state-space shape, different basis: global modes diagonalise the physics, while local grid samples preserve locality.

When to Use Which

Modal

  • Linear, time-invariant systems.
  • Sparse spectra (a few hundred modes).
  • Per-mode damping is trivial.
  • Output at arbitrary positions: just sample \(\phi_\mu(x_\text{out})\).
  • Embarrassingly parallel (decoupled ODEs).
  • Awkward for distributed nonlinearity or contact.

FDTD

  • Local interactions: contact, collisions, distributed nonlinearity.
  • Complex geometries (irregular domains).
  • Sparse banded \(\mathbf A\), locality is natural.
  • Per-grid-point physics: easy to introduce position-dependent material.
  • Stability is a constraint (CFL).
  • Dispersion errors for high modes.

Both produce \(\mathbf h_{n+1} = \mathbf A\, \mathbf h_n + \mathbf b\, f_n\). The state-space machinery and downstream learning hooks are basis-agnostic.

Next week: nonlinearities make modal awkward (mode coupling everywhere). FDTD shines here, but neural methods can make nonlinearities tractable in the modal domain.
